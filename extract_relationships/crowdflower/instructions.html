# last updated 2015-03-19 toby Title: Determining the relationships between concepts

Overview

Biomedical literature contains a wealth of information that remains locked in a text based format. In order to be able to effectively search all this knowledge, the information needs to be represented in a structured way. In this task you will be helping scientists organize biomedical knowlege by linking together concepts with structured relationships.

The Task

  1. Read a sentence from a real scientific article. Pay special attention to the two highlighted concepts in the sentence.
  2. You will be told what sort of concept the two highlighted terms are. The possible categories are: diseases, drugs, genes, and variants of genes. Definitions of each of these types of things are provided below.
  3. You will need to determine whether the two highlighted concepts are related according to the sentence. Concepts can be joined by four kinds of relationships, which are defined below.
  4. If the sentence claims that the two concepts are related, then you will be asked to expand on what the sentence says about how these two ideas are related. The kinds of possible relationships between concepts will depend on what category each concept belongs to (drug, gene, disease, or gene variant). Please choose the most specific answer that faithfully represents the information found in the sentence.

Making your judgement

  • Please base your answers completely upon what is explicitly stated in the provided sentence. For this task, we are only trying to provide an accurate representation of the sentence itself. We are not looking for "absolute truth". Therefore if you know the sentence itself is wrong, then please complete the task according to the sentence and leave us a comment.

Categories that concepts can belong to

Gene or Protein

What genes and proteins are:
  • Genes are parts of DNA molecules. Every living organism contains DNA.
  • Genes contain information. This information is used to make proteins. A gene and the protein it makes are often referred to interchangeably.
  • Proteins are molecules that do things in living organisms. For example, there are proteins that digest food and repair cuts. Proteins are made from amino acids. If proteins are big Lego houses, then amino acids are the individual Lego pieces.
  • When a gene is used to make a protein, we say that the gene "expresses" the protein.
Why genes and proteins are important:
  • An organism's genes determine the characteristics of that organism, such as how big it is, or how it looks.
  • Genes are heritable. An organism's genes will be similar to those of its parents.
  • Genes can change (be mutated). When a gene is mutated, it can cause a disease. Changes in genes result in changes in proteins, because the information needed to make proteins are stored in genes.
Examples of genes and proteins (highlighted) in text:
  • Most of the glial growth inhibiting factors converge onto the ROCK signaling pathway in neurons.
  • Here, we report on MUC1 overexpression in HCC cell lines.
  • Results: The chemokine landscape of ovarian cancer is heterogeneous with high expression of known lymphocyte-recruiting chemokines in tumors with intraepithelial T cells, whereas CXCL10 is expressed quasi-universally, including in tumors lacking tumor infiltrating T cells.

Gene variant

What gene variants are:
  • Genes can change, or "mutate". What this means is that the information contained in the gene has been altered.
  • If a gene is a word like "cat", then you can think of a mutation as changes to the letters of the word. For example, we can mutate "cat" by making changing "c" to "h" to get "hat". Only one letter has changed, but the idea that the three letters represent has changed drastically! Small changes to genes can cause similarly large changes in meaning.
  • These changes to genes are referred to as "gene variants". This is because the changes are all variations to the normal version of the gene.
  • Gene variants have many different types, because the original gene can be altered in many ways. For example, genes can have bits added to them (insertions), portions removed (deletions), or the letters changed (mutations).
Why gene variants are important:
  • Some gene variants can cause disease. Certain changes in important genes can cause disease. Whether or not a gene variant causes a disease depends upon the type of change and the gene that it affects.
  • Gene variants can be used to identify individuals. For example, detectives can use gene variants to determine who committed a crime.
Examples of gene variants (highlighted) in text:
  • Interferon-induced transmembrane protein-3 rs12252-C is associated with rapid progression of acute HIV-1 infection in Chinese MSM cohort.
  • Haplotype analysis revealed significant association of G(rs10509681)-*1(rs1799853)-*3(rs1057910)-G(rs4244285) on chromosome 10 with overall toxicity (P=0.024) and grade 2-4 leucopenia (P=0.03). (There are four different variants in this sentence.)
  • The study detected no significant differences among the carriers of various genotypes at polymorphic markers 862TC and 2121GA of CLOCK gene and 56445TC of BMAL1 gene.

Drug

What drugs are:
  • Drugs are molecules.
  • Many different kinds of molecules can be used as drugs. For example, chicken noodle soup can be called a drug because it is given to people with colds to help them feel better.
  • Proteins can also be used as drugs. For example, botox is a drug that is made from proteins. It is used to make skin look younger.
Why drugs are important:
  • Drugs are used to treat diseases. For example, penicillin has saved millions of lives by curing bacterial infections.
  • Drugs can make people feel better. For example, Prozac treats depression by making people happier.
Examples of drugs (highlighted) in text:
  • Evaluation of 2,6-dichlorophenolindophenol acetate as a substrate for acetylcholinesterase activity assay.
  • Using human NT2 model neurons, we demonstrate that the pain reliever Ibuprofen decreases RhoA (Ras homolog gene family, member A GTPase) activation and promotes neurite growth.
  • Inhibition of the downstream effector Rho kinase by the drug Y-27632 results in a strong increase in neurite outgrowth.

Disease

What diseases are:
  • Diseases are abnormal conditions of a living organism.
  • Diseases can be caused by illness, poisons, or external factors like viruses or bacteria.
Why diseases are important:
  • Diseases make people unhappy and unproductive.
  • Diseases are a big resource drain for a society, because it prevents people from working.
Examples of diseases (highlighted) in text:
  • Folliculitis decalvans leads to scarring alopecia through inflammatory destruction of the hair follicle.
  • This case report regards pregnancy and delivery of a patient who had undergone proctocolectomy and ileo-pouch-anal-anastomosis (IPAA) for ulcerative colitis.
  • Language Development in Infants and Toddlers With Fragile X Syndrome: Change Over Time and the Role of Attention.

Definitions of the four kinds of relationships

For this task, two concepts can be related in four possible ways:

  1. Definite positive relationship
  2. Definite negative relationship
  3. Definite speculative relationship
  4. Unknown relationship

Definite positive relationship

Definition: the given sentence clearly states that there is an association between the two concepts.

Examples:
  • Consider the sentence: Mutations in breast cancer susceptibility gene BRCA1 (breast cancer early-onset 1) are associated with increased risk of developing breast and ovarian cancers.
  • The sentence above clearly states that mutations in the gene "BRCA1" cause higher risk of developing "breast and ovarian cancers". Since the sentence says that there is a link between "BRCA1" and "breast and ovarian cancers", the two concepts "BRCA1" and "breast and ovarian cancers" are related by a definite positive relationship.
  • Consider the sentence: All hamburger patties are made from chicken.
  • In the above sentence, "hamburger patties" and "chicken" share a definite positive relationship because the "hamburger patties" are made from "chicken". Although you know that hamburgers are made from beef, you should not say that there is no relationship between "chicken" and "hamburger patties". For the purpose of this task, please focus on representing what the sentence is trying to say, regardless of whether you know the information presented in that sentence is false. Therefore, in this sentence, "hamburger patties" and "chicken" share a definite positive relationship because the sentence says so, even though we all know that hamburgers are made from beef.

Definite negative relationship

Definition: the given sentence clearly states that there is not an association between the two concepts.

Examples:
  • Consider the sentence: We failed to find any evidence that eating prunes causes athlete's foot based on a large-scale study of athletes.
  • The above sentence states that there was no evidence suggesting that eating prunes causes athlete's foot. Therefore, "prunes" and "athlete's foot" are related by a definite negative relationship. If you know that prunes definitely cause athlete's foot, then you still need to say that prunes and athelete's foot are related by a definite negative relationship, because that is what the sentence is saying.
  • Consider the sentence: The world is definitely not round. In this sentence, there is a definite negative relationship between "world" and "round", because the sentence says so. Everyone today knows that the Earth is round, but this is not something you know based on the given sentence. We have no way of knowing whether the Earth is actually round based on this sentence. Therefore, we can only clearly represent what the sentence is saying, which is that there is a definite negative relationship between "world" and "round".

Definite speculative relationship

Definition: the given sentence clearly states that there might be an association between the two concepts.

Examples:
  • Consider the sentence: Based on our research, we think that running marathons may lead to knee injury. This sentence says that "running marathons" may lead to "knee injury". Therefore the two concepts of "running marathons" and "knee injury" are related by a definite speculative relationship, because the sentence says so. You might know that running definitely causes knee injury, and therefore think that the two concpets should be joined by a definite positive relationship; however, there is nothing in the sentence which says that the link is definitive. Since the sentence never says whether the link is real, the two concepts do not share a definite positive relationship, but rather a definite speculative relationship.
  • Consider the sentence: After John drank three beers, he tried to drive home, but crashed into a tree before he reached his house. What is the relationship between "three beers" and "crashing into a tree"? Based on this sentence, you might conclude that John had too much alcohol to drink before driving, which impaired his decision making and therefore caused him to crash into a tree. However, nothing in the sentence suggests that this is true. The sentence is merely recounting what John did: he drank some beer, he drove his car, and he crashed into a tree. Nothing in this sentence says that the beer might have caused John to crash into the tree. This is inferred by the reader, and is not explicitly stated. Therefore, "three beers" and "crashing into a tree" do not share a definite speculative relationship. In this sentence, there is an unknown relationship between "three beers" and "crashing into a tree", because the sentence never talks about how and if the two concepts are related.

Unknown relationship

Definition: the sentence does not explicitly state whether the two concepts are related.

Examples:
  • Consider the sentence: In this paper, we examined the link between drinking alcohol and the incidence of traffic accidents. In this sentence, there is an unknown relationship between "drinking alcohol" and "traffic accidents". The sentence does not clearly say that "drinking alcohol" causes "traffic accidents", and is therefore not a definite positive relationship. The sentence also does not say that "drinking alcohol" may cause "traffic accidents", and therefore there is no definite speculative relationship. You might argue that if there was no link between "drinking alcohol" and "traffic accidents", then the scientists would not have bothered to study it in the first place. While this may be true in reality, there is nothing to suggest that the two ideas are linked in the sentence. The sentence is only saying that the scientists looked at whether there was a link between "drinking alcohol" and "traffic accidents". Whether they found a positive link, a speculative link, or a negative link, is not known based on this sentence! Therefore, the two concepts are linked with an unknown relationship.

Background information

This task was created by the Su Lab at The Scripps Research Institute. If you have any questions about the task, please do not hesitate to email your questions to tongli@scripps.edu

Scientists all over the world are generating too much knowledge. Every day, more and more scientific articles are published in fields like biology and chemistry. In fact, the rate at which this information is produced greatly exceeds the ability of individual scientists to absorb all this information. You can think of the information being produced as a great river; individual scientists can only ever drink a small portion of this great river of knowledge.

This is a problem, because scientists make decisions and perform experiments based on what they know. Since it is impossible for a single scientist to know everything, it is very possible that an experiment a scientist wants to perform, or a hypothesis that a scientist wants to test, has already been done, or is already known to be false.

Since science is an expensive endeavour, we want to perform the most interesting experiments, those which generate new knowledge. These experiments are most likely to create breakthroughs like those which lead to modern medicines and technology. In order to do this, scientists require tools to help them stay informed and up-to-date.

Scientists require tools which will be able to summarize large portions of scientific knowledge into structured pieces of information that the researcher can use to inform his or her decisions. Computer programs are an obvious choice for working through large amounts of information, because they work quickly. Many research groups are working on computer programs that would automatically read scientific articles and create pieces of knowledge from these articles. For example, a computer may read an article about how smoking can cause lung cancer, and extract the information: "[smoking] [causes] [lung cancer]". The extracted information is usually structured: as in, two concepts (smoking and lung cancer) are connected somehow ("causes").

However, you can imagine that this is a difficult task to do, especially for computers, because human language and scientific papers are nuanced and complex. This is where crowdsourcing comes in. We believe that groups of non-specialist workers can determine the relationships between concepts as well as, and even better than, specialists in field. Since non-specialists use patterns to deal with information they are unfamiliar with, we think that these patterns can be taught to computers, and therefore be used to create the automated tools scientists of the future will use. In this task, you will be helping us create these patterns by determining the details of how concepts are related to one another.
